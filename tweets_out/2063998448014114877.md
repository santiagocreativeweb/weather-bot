### Where the Edge Comes From

The biggest mistake most people make in prediction markets is simple:

They follow the crowd.

If someone buys 21°C, they buy 21°C too.

If someone sells a position at a loss, they panic and copy the move.

If the market starts moving, they assume the market knows something.

But the market is not always right.

The price you see on Polymarket is not the truth.

It is the opinion of people trading at that moment.

And people are often emotional.

They overreact.
They follow others.
They buy late.
They sell too early.
They enter without a plan.

That is where the edge appears.

The edge is not in following the crowd.

The edge is in doing your own analysis before the crowd sees the setup.

![img_2](2063998448014114877_media\img_2.com)

In weather markets, this matters a lot.

Sometimes the best trade is the one nobody talks about yet.

You enter early.

You wait.

Then the market slowly starts to understand what you already saw in the data.

That is the difference between a random trader and a weather trader with a system.

### The Biggest Mistakes Beginners Make

Most beginners lose money for the same reasons.

They follow the market.

They do not analyze the forecast.

They trade without a strategy.

They enter too late.

They panic when price moves against them.

They treat prediction markets like gambling.

And I understand this very well, because I made many of these mistakes too.

When I started trading weather markets, I also lost money.

I also did not always understand which forecast to trust.

I also looked at different models and saw completely different numbers.

One model says Seoul will reach 25°C.

Another says 26°C.

Another says rain will come and the maximum temperature will be only 21°C.

So the question becomes:

Who should you trust?

The answer is not simple.

Because every weather model has strengths and weaknesses.

Some models are better for precipitation.

Some are better for maximum temperature.

Some are more reliable in specific cities.

Some perform badly when there is a sharp temperature change.

And some models are simply not useful for certain markets.

This is where my journey started.

### From Problem to Product

I created Weather Bot because I needed it myself.

At first, I was tired of checking many websites manually.

I had to compare different models.

Look at temperature.
Look at humidity.
Look at pressure.
Look at wind.
Check the trend.
Check stability.
Check if the market price made sense.

It took too much time.

And even after doing all of that, the result was not always clear.

So I started building a system.

![img_3](2063998448014114877_media\img_3.com)

The first version was simple.

I collected different weather models and tested them on specific cities.

Then I compared their predictions with real outcomes.

Very quickly, I saw one important thing:

Different cities require different models.

There is no universal forecast model that works perfectly everywhere.

A model that performs well in London may not be the best for Seoul.

A model that works for Tokyo may not be ideal for Munich.

A model that is useful for pressure may not be the best for maximum temperature.

So I started testing city by city.

Model by model.

Forecast by forecast.

Trade by trade.

Later, I added more advanced and paid weather sources.

I also looked deeper into which models had better performance for specific locations.

The goal was not to create a “magic signal.”

The goal was to create a faster and cleaner way to analyze weather markets.
